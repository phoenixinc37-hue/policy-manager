**The Corporate Structure**

A corporation in Canada operates through three distinct levels. Each has defined rights, responsibilities and limitations.

**1. Shareholders**
Shareholders own equity in the corporation. Their rights are limited to ownership-level decisions: electing directors, approving major structural changes (amalgamations, dissolutions), and reviewing annual financial statements.

Shareholders do not have the right to direct day-to-day operations, override management decisions, or instruct employees. Ownership does not equal operational authority. This applies regardless of the size of a shareholder's ownership stake. A majority shareholder holds no greater operational authority than a minority shareholder. Majority ownership does not grant the right to direct employees, override the CEO, or interfere with operational decisions. Those rights do not exist at the shareholder level — majority or otherwise.

**2. Directors**
Directors are elected by shareholders to govern the corporation at a strategic level. Directors carry a fiduciary duty — a legal obligation to act in the best interest of the corporation, not in personal interest.

Directors set policy and strategic direction. They do not manage operations directly. A director acting outside their governance role — directing employees, overriding management — is acting outside their authority.

**3. Officers / Executive Management**
Officers (including the CEO) are appointed by the Board to manage the corporation's day-to-day operations. The CEO has the authority and responsibility to make operational decisions, direct staff, implement strategy, and manage the business.

Operational decisions — staffing, pricing, tools, procedures — fall within CEO authority. These decisions do not require shareholder approval.

---

**What This Means in Practice**

- Operational instructions flow from the CEO and management team
- Directors provide governance oversight — not operational direction
- Employees take direction from management, not from shareholders or directors acting outside their governance role
- A director who disagrees with an operational decision has the right to raise it at the board level — not to countermand it directly

---

**Fiduciary Duty and Director Liability**

Every director carries a legal obligation to act in the best interest of the corporation. This is not optional and it is not passive.

As our accountant has outlined, the financial position of this business requires immediate and decisive action. Failure to act is not a neutral position — it is a decision with real financial consequences.

Directors who fail to meet their fiduciary duty can face serious personal liability under Canadian corporate law. This includes:

- **Personal financial liability** — directors can be held personally responsible for losses the corporation suffers as a result of their failure to act or breach of fiduciary duty
- **Liability for unpaid obligations** — under the Alberta Business Corporations Act, directors can be held personally liable for unpaid wages, source deductions, and certain tax remittances
- **Oppression remedy** — a director acting in a manner that is oppressive or unfairly prejudicial to the interests of the corporation can be subject to court-ordered remedy, including removal
- **CRA liability** — directors can be held personally liable for unremitted HST/GST and payroll source deductions
- **Going concern liability** — where a corporation fails as a going concern and a director's actions or inaction contributed to that failure, personal liability exposure increases significantly

**Accountability and Execution**

Decisions and direction come from the CEO. The expectation is that they are acted on — not debated, delayed, or deflected.

The business is in a position where every week of inaction has a measurable cost. Time and energy spent defending plans, responding to obstruction, or counteracting decisions already made is time not spent on execution. That is not acceptable given where we are.

Going forward:
- CEO direction is to be implemented unless a legitimate governance concern is raised through the appropriate channel
- Raising questions or concerns does not suspend implementation
- Accountability for execution sits with the individuals responsible for each area
- Non-compliance with operational direction will be addressed directly

The expectation is execution. Not emails. Not delays. Execution.
