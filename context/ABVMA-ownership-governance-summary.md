## The Core Distinction

There are two separate concepts that are often conflated but are legally distinct:

1. **Share Ownership Requirements** — who can own shares of a veterinary corporation
2. **Responsible Veterinarian** — who holds clinical authority over veterinary medicine

These are not the same thing. Ownership does not equal operational control of the business.

---

## 1. Share Ownership — What the Rules Actually Say

Under ABVMA regulations and the Alberta Veterinary Profession Act:

> *"All shares of a Veterinary Professional Corporation must be owned either directly by a licensed veterinarian or through a holding company whose shareholders are all licensed veterinarians. Family members who are not licensed veterinarians may not be shareholders of a VPC."*
> — KPK Law (Veterinary Professional Corporations: The Ins and Outs)

**What this means for CSI:**
- Tammy Wilde (DVM) holds the majority of voting shares — this is required because she is the licensed veterinarian
- Scott Wilde holds shares through a permissible structure
- The ownership requirement exists to ensure a licensed veterinarian maintains an ownership stake — **it is not a grant of operational authority**

The 51%+ majority share requirement exists for **regulatory compliance purposes only** — to satisfy ABVMA's requirement that a licensed vet holds controlling ownership. It does not transfer CEO or operational authority to the shareholder.

---

## 2. Responsible Veterinarian — What the Role Actually Covers

> *"For any Veterinary Practice Entity, a 'Responsible Veterinarian' must be designated. This individual must be an unrestricted registered veterinarian appointed by the VPE owner. The Responsible Veterinarian holds the ultimate authority over the VPE, is appropriately informed about all aspects of the veterinary practice, and acts as the overall decision-maker regarding its operations, ensuring compliance with the Veterinary Profession Act, General Regulation, ABVMA Bylaws, and all guidelines. The Responsible Veterinarian is accountable for all aspects of the practice of veterinary medicine conducted by a permit holder."*
> — ABVMA Bylaws / Regulatory Framework

**What "ultimate authority" means in this context:**
The Responsible Veterinarian's authority is specifically scoped to:
- Patient care decisions
- Clinical standards and protocols
- Compliance with the Veterinary Profession Act
- Professional conduct of registered veterinarians at the facility

**What it does NOT cover:**
- Staffing decisions (hiring, firing, HR)
- Pricing and fee schedules
- IT systems and software implementation
- Financial management and banking
- Supplier and vendor relationships
- CEO and operational authority under the Alberta Business Corporations Act

---

## 3. The Key Legal Boundary

The ABVMA framework is designed to prevent **non-clinicians from interfering with professional veterinary judgment**. It is not designed to give veterinarians operational control over the business entity that employs them or that they co-own.

The Alberta Business Corporations Act governs corporate operational authority. Under that framework:
- A CEO holds operational authority over the business
- A majority shareholder holds voting rights on shareholder matters (major corporate decisions, director appointments)
- A director holds fiduciary duties to act in the best interest of the corporation

**These are separate authorities that can — and in CSI's case do — reside in different people.**

---

## 4. Practical Summary

| Role | Who | Scope |
|------|-----|-------|
| Majority Shareholder | Tammy Wilde | Ownership votes, major corporate decisions |
| Responsible Veterinarian | Tammy Wilde | Clinical operations, patient care, ABVMA compliance |
| CEO / Operations | Scott Wilde | Day-to-day business management, staffing, pricing, IT, financial oversight |
| Director | Both | Fiduciary duty to act in best interest of CSI |

The fact that Tammy holds majority shares and is the Responsible Veterinarian does not make her the CEO or give her operational authority over the business. Those are separate roles governed by separate legal frameworks.

---

## 5. Why This Matters Now

The letter from Brownlee LLP (April 3, 2026) conflates these roles — suggesting that Tammy's clinical and ownership role gives her authority over "operational direction of the clinics, including staff, patient care, and day-to-day management."

This conflation is legally inaccurate. Operational direction of the business entity is a CEO function under the ABCA — not a shareholder or Responsible Veterinarian function under the ABVMA.

A clear governance report from corporate counsel establishing these boundaries is the appropriate mechanism to resolve this ambiguity.
